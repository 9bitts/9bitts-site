Leonard Cohen · Persistir mesmo sem esperanca de resultado

The Work That Asks Nothing Back

Leonard Cohen spent years writing songs nobody heard. That was not the beginning of his career. That was the whole point.

Leonard Cohen spent more than a decade writing a single song. Not because he was slow. Because he believed the song demanded it. He revised Hallelujah hundreds of times across different decades, adding verses, removing them, starting over. The song had no guaranteed audience. He kept going anyway. That is the part that deserves more attention.

There is a kind of persistence that runs on hope. You keep working because you believe something good is coming. That is reasonable and human. But Cohen seemed to practice something different. A persistence that does not require the outcome to justify the effort. The work is done because the work is the thing, not the reward waiting after it.

This is not the same as despair. Despair stops. What Cohen modeled was continuation under uncertainty, which is a different posture entirely. He also spent years in a Zen monastery on Mount Baldy, waking before dawn, following a routine with no promise of enlightenment. Not because discipline would fix him. Because showing up, repeatedly, without expectation, was itself a form of integrity.

Most creative culture runs on metrics. Streams, reach, validation. The question underneath everything has become: is it working? Cohen asked something harder. Is it true? That shift in question changes everything about how you make things and how you live inside the making. It removes the audience from the center and puts the work there instead.

Cohen released his final album three weeks before he died. He had known he was close to the end. He finished it anyway. Not for legacy. Not for closure. The finishing was just what came next. That might be the quietest and most radical thing a person can do: continue not because the future looks good, but because the present still asks something of you.
