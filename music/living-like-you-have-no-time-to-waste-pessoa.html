Living Like You Have No Time to Waste

Fernando Pessoa spent his life multiplying selves, yet somewhere beneath the masks, a single urgency pulsed: do not let the hours dissolve into nothing.

Pessoa worked as a commercial translator by day, wrote under borrowed names by night, and died leaving a trunk packed with thousands of unpublished pages. That trunk is not a symbol of failure. It is a record of someone who refused to let a single hour go unexamined.

The Portuguese phrase viver como quem não tem tempo a perder does not translate cleanly. It is not hustle culture dressed in literary clothes. It is closer to a discipline of attention: treating each moment as one you cannot afford to sleepwalk through, because it will not return in any form.

In the Book of Disquiet, Bernardo Soares, one of Pessoa's heteronyms, watches Lisbon from a window and turns the act of watching into literature. He is not productive in any measurable sense. But he is completely present. That presence is the practice. The output is almost secondary.

Most of what we call wasting time is not laziness. It is the slow erosion of intention. We scroll not because we want to but because wanting requires effort, and we have quietly stopped making that effort. Pessoa's life, fragmented and eccentric as it was, reads as a sustained argument against that erosion.

To live without wasting time is not to be busy. It is to know, with some clarity, what you are doing and why you are doing it. Even sitting still can qualify, if the stillness is chosen. What does not qualify is the foggy middle state where hours pass and you cannot account for a single one.

Pessoa never became famous in his lifetime. The trunk waited. But the work was done, page after page, because something in him understood that the time given to a person is finite and specific, and that squandering it is not a minor failure. It is the only failure that is truly irreversible.
