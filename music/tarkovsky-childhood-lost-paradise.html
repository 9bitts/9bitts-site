Andrei Tarkovsky · A infancia como paraiso perdido e recuperavel

The Country You Can Never Stop Leaving

Tarkovsky believed childhood was not something behind us but something we keep failing to return to. This is about that distance, and what it costs.

There is a specific ache that comes not from loss but from proximity. You can almost touch it. A particular light through a window, the smell of wet earth after rain, someone calling your name from another room. Childhood does not disappear. It becomes unreachable while remaining completely present.

Tarkovsky returned to his childhood constantly, not as sentiment but as method. In his films, memory is not illustration. It is the actual substance of time. What he understood is that the child does not experience the world as a stage in development. The child experiences it as the whole world. Adults spend the rest of their lives negotiating that loss.

The paradise of childhood is not really about happiness. It is about wholeness. Before the self becomes something to manage, before time becomes something to spend, there is a mode of being in which experience and awareness are not yet separate. Art, at its most serious, tries to recover that. Not to describe it from outside but to recreate the condition itself.

This is where the question of recoverability becomes complicated. You cannot go back. But the impulse to return is not delusional. It points toward something real, a capacity for presence that existed before self-consciousness dulled it. The work of art, the serious kind, does not give you nostalgia. It gives you access. Briefly. Enough to remember what full attention feels like.

Maybe childhood is less a time than a posture toward the world. One that adulthood trains out of us, slowly, with good reasons. And maybe what we call longing is actually practice. The repeated attempt to inhabit something we never fully lost, only learned to look past. The country is still there. We just keep moving in the wrong direction.
