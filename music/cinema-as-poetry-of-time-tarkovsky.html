Andrei Tarkovsky · O cinema como poesia do tempo

Cinema Is Time You Can Hold

Tarkovsky believed film was not about images or stories. It was about sculpting time itself, and that changes how you watch everything.

Most people watch a film waiting for something to happen. Andrei Tarkovsky thought that was the wrong posture entirely. For him, the event was already occurring in the duration, in the held breath of a long take, in the way water moves or wind crosses a field. The film was not delivering content. It was constructing an experience of time.

Tarkovsky wrote in his book Sculpting in Time that cinema's distinct capacity is to record and reproduce time. Not story, not beauty, not spectacle. Time. Each art form, he argued, has its own material. For painting it is color and form. For music, sound across duration. For cinema, it is time imprinted on a strip of celluloid, preserved and made repeatable.

This is why his films resist summary. Stalker, Mirror, Nostalghia are not structured around plot momentum. They move the way memory moves, associatively, with weight that doesn't always explain itself. You are not meant to decode them. You are meant to pass through them, the way you pass through a place that changes you without offering a reason.

The idea has consequences beyond film. If time itself is the raw material, then pace is not pacing, it is the texture of existence rendered visible. A slow scene is not slow. It is honest about how certain moments actually feel when you are inside them. Speed, in this framework, is often a way of avoiding presence.

What Tarkovsky gives you, if you stay with him, is a recalibration. You start to notice how often art, and life, rushes past its own substance. He was not making slow films as a style choice. He was insisting that time, felt deeply and without distraction, is itself the thing worth preserving. That stays with you after the screen goes dark.
