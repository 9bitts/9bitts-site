Andrei Tarkovsky · A infancia como paraiso perdido e recuperavel

The Country You Can Never Stop Leaving

Tarkovsky believed childhood was not something you grew out of but something you spent a lifetime trying to return to. The question is whether that return is possible, or whether the attempt itself is the point.

There is a moment in Tarkovsky's Mirror where a woman stands in a field and wind moves through the tall grass and nothing happens, narratively speaking. Yet something in the body responds. Not nostalgia exactly. Something older. A recognition of a place you were once from and can no longer locate on any map.

For Tarkovsky, childhood was not a biographical phase. It was a mode of being. A state in which time moves differently, in which the world is still thick with meaning and the self has not yet learned to edit its own wonder. Losing that was not growing up. It was a kind of exile.

His films keep returning to the same imagery. Rain on wooden floors. A sleeping child. Water moving over submerged objects. These are not decorations. They are attempts at recovery. The camera lingers not to be beautiful but to recreate the duration of childhood attention, that specific quality of noticing without agenda.

What he understood, and what most theories of nostalgia miss, is that the loss is not about the past as it was. It is about a self that perceived differently. You do not mourn the summer house or the grandmother's kitchen. You mourn the version of you who could be fully inside those rooms without thinking about being inside them.

Whether that can be recovered is the wrong question. Art, at its most honest, does not promise return. It offers something stranger: the sudden knowledge, while watching light cross a wall or hearing a particular silence, that the thing you thought was gone has been inside you the whole time, waiting for the right quality of attention to find it again.
