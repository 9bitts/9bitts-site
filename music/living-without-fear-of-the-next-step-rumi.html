Rumi · Viver sem medo do proximo passo

The Reed Knows Nothing About the Shore It Left

Rumi spent his life writing about separation not as tragedy but as the very condition that makes longing possible. What if the next step doesn't need to be safe to be right?

There is a moment before any real decision where the mind goes very quiet and then very loud. You know the one. The step is right there. And something in you manufactures a thousand reasons to wait one more day. That hesitation is not wisdom. It is usually just fear wearing the costume of caution.

Rumi opens the Masnavi with the image of a reed flute crying because it has been cut from its reed bed. That separation is painful. It is also the only reason the reed can make music at all. The wound and the song are the same thing. You cannot have one without agreeing to the other.

What people call fear of the next step is often something more specific. It is the fear of becoming someone you do not yet recognize. Moving forward requires a small death of the version of you that stayed still. That is genuinely disorienting. Rumi does not pretend otherwise. His poetry does not comfort you out of the feeling. It asks you to go deeper into it.

There is a particular kind of suffering that comes not from what life does to you but from the constant negotiation between what you know you need to do and what you keep postponing. That negotiation is exhausting in a way that is hard to name. It lives in the body. It disrupts sleep. It makes ordinary things feel heavy without any obvious reason.

The reed does not know what music it will make when the breath moves through it. It only knows the hollow place where the cutting happened and the strange fact that this is exactly what makes it useful. Living without fear of the next step does not mean living without fear. It means taking the step anyway and trusting that the hollow place in you is not a flaw. It is the opening.
