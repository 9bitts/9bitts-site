01 — Origins

Where it began

Electronic music is not a genre. It is a method — the use of electronic technology to generate, modify, or record sound. What began as avant-garde experimentation in European universities in the 1940s became, by the 1990s, the defining soundtrack of global youth culture. The path between those two points is one of the most unlikely stories in the history of music.

The first machines were not built for clubs. They were built for laboratories. Composers like Pierre Schaeffer in Paris and Karlheinz Stockhausen in Cologne were interested in the philosophical problem of sound itself — what it meant to record reality and reassemble it. Musique concrète, the school Schaeffer founded in 1948, was the first systematic attempt to make music from recorded sound rather than performed sound. The tape recorder was the instrument.

1948

Musique Concrète — Paris

Pierre Schaeffer experiments with recorded sounds at RTF studios. "Étude aux chemins de fer" — made from train sounds — becomes the first widely recognized piece of electronic music.

1964

The Moog Synthesizer

Robert Moog introduces voltage-controlled synthesis, making electronic sound production available outside institutional studios. Music changes permanently.

1974

Kraftwerk — Autobahn

Four musicians from Düsseldorf release an album that sounds like nothing before it. Kraftwerk becomes the unlikely origin point of almost every genre in this guide.

1980

Roland TR-808

A drum machine considered a commercial failure becomes the backbone of hip-hop, house, and techno. Its bass drum is still one of the most recognizable sounds in contemporary music.

1986

Chicago House

At The Warehouse and Music Box, DJs Frankie Knuckles and Ron Hardy play a new kind of music assembled from disco, soul, and drum machines. They call it house. The world follows.

1988

Detroit Techno & the Second Summer of Love

Juan Atkins, Derrick May, and Kevin Saunderson release what becomes the Detroit techno canon. Simultaneously, MDMA and Roland 303s transform British warehouse raves. Two movements, one cultural rupture.

1990s

Berlin After the Wall

The fall of the Berlin Wall opens abandoned buildings. Tresor opens in a former bank vault in 1991. Berghain opens in 2004. A city becomes the global capital of techno.

2000s–now

Melodic Techno & the Digital Era

Artists like Tale Of Us, Anyma, and Massano develop a more melodic, emotionally open strand of techno. Streaming and social media reshape how electronic music is discovered and consumed.

02 — Genres

The landscape of sound

Electronic music is not one thing. It is a family of related approaches to rhythm, texture, and time — each with its own geography, its own tempo, its own emotional register. What follows is not exhaustive. It is a map of the territory most relevant to the 9bitts universe.

~120–130 BPM

House

Born in Chicago in the early 1980s from the ruins of disco. Four-on-the-floor kick drum, soulful vocals, chord stabs. The most democratic of electronic genres — built to make people move together.

Chicago, 1983 · Frankie Knuckles, Larry Heard
~130–150 BPM

Techno

Invented in Detroit as a sonic response to deindustrialization. Harder, more mechanical, more repetitive than house. Designed for endurance and transformation. The music of long nights.

Detroit, 1986 · Juan Atkins, Derrick May, Kevin Saunderson
~128–135 BPM

Melodic Techno

A relatively recent evolution — techno's structure with deeper melodic and harmonic content. Emotional, often cinematic. The genre closest to the 9bitts aesthetic.

Italy/Germany, 2010s · Tale Of Us, Anyma, Massano
~124–128 BPM

Deep House

Slower, warmer, more introspective than regular house. Jazz chords, lush pads, understated rhythms. Music for the early morning or the late night — the hours when the room empties but a few remain.

Chicago/New York, late 1980s · Larry Heard, Frankie Knuckles
~125–132 BPM

Progressive House

Built around gradual evolution rather than sudden drops. Long builds, slow filters, a sense of perpetual motion. Influenced Deadmau5, Eric Prydz, and a generation of stadium-scale producers.

UK, early 1990s · Sasha, John Digweed
~136–145 BPM

Trance

Defined by hypnotic, layered synthesis and extended breakdowns designed to produce altered states. From German techno-trance of the 1990s to the modern psytrance scene — a vast and still-evolving territory.

Germany, early 1990s · Paul van Dyk, Sven Väth
~160–180 BPM

Drum and Bass

Breakbeats running at extreme speed over deep sub-bass. Born in London's rave underground. The genre that most directly confronted the raw physics of rhythm.

London, early 1990s · Goldie, LTJ Bukem, Roni Size
Variable BPM

Ambient

Brian Eno codified it in 1978: music that could be ignored or actively listened to, and work in both modes. The anti-genre — defined by absence as much as presence. Essential context for understanding everything else.

UK, 1978 · Brian Eno, Harold Budd
~130–140 BPM

Minimal Techno

Stripped of everything non-essential. A single element, repeated with slight variation over long stretches of time. What remains when you remove the melody, the harmony, and most of the rhythm.

Berlin, late 1990s · Ricardo Villalobos, Richie Hawtin
03 — Equipment

The machines that made it

Electronic music is inseparable from its instruments. Unlike acoustic music, where the instruments predate the music by centuries, electronic music and its machines evolved together — each new device opening a set of sonic possibilities that composers and producers raced to explore. These are the machines that defined the sound.

Roland TR-808
1980
A programmable drum machine considered a commercial failure on release — too artificial, too unlike real drums. Within a decade it became the foundation of hip-hop, house, and techno. Its kick drum is the most recognizable sound in contemporary popular music. Still in use everywhere.
Roland TB-303
1981
Designed to simulate a bass guitar for solo practice. Failed at its intended purpose. Discovered by Chicago producers who found that certain settings produced an alien, liquid, squelching sound unlike anything else. That sound became acid house. The 303 remains one of the most influential objects in music history.
Moog Synthesizer
1964
Robert Moog's voltage-controlled synthesizer democratized electronic sound production. Wendy Carlos's "Switched-On Bach" (1968) proved to a skeptical public that electronic instruments could produce music of serious artistic merit. Everything after it carries Moog's DNA.
Akai MPC60
1988
A sampling drum machine and sequencer that became central to hip-hop production and electronic music. The MPC's workflow — pads, sequences, samples — shaped how producers think about rhythm to this day.
Pioneer CDJ
1994
The CD DJ player that became the global standard for club performance. CDJs redefined what DJing meant — moving the practice from vinyl expertise toward real-time composition. The current CDJ-3000 is the industry standard in virtually every serious club on earth.
Ableton Live
2001
Software that changed the relationship between production and performance. Ableton's session view made real-time arrangement possible. It became the tool of choice for a generation of electronic producers and live performers — including most of the artists shaping the current melodic techno scene.
04 — Cities

Where the music lives

Electronic music is not geography-neutral. Its key developments happened in specific places, at specific moments, under specific social conditions. Cities matter — not as scenery but as cause. Detroit's deindustrialisation, Chicago's Black gay nightlife, Berlin's post-Wall vacancy: these were the soil in which the music grew.

USA

Detroit

The birthplace of techno. Juan Atkins, Derrick May, and Kevin Saunderson — the Belleville Three — created the genre as a response to economic collapse. Techno was their vision of a Black futurism that American capitalism had failed to deliver.

USA

Chicago

Where house was born. The Warehouse, the Music Box, the Power Plant — these were Black and Latino gay clubs where DJs built a new form from disco's ruins. House music was a survival culture before it was a global phenomenon.

Germany

Berlin

The fall of the Wall in 1989 left a city full of empty space. Artists and DJs moved into abandoned buildings. Tresor (1991), Berghain (2004), Watergate — Berlin's clubs became institutions. The city remains the global reference point for serious techno culture.

Spain

Ibiza

The Mediterranean island that became synonymous with summer electronic music. Amnesia, Pacha, DC-10 — clubs that shaped the sound and business of dance music globally. Complex, contradictory, and still essential.

UK

Manchester & London

The Haçienda in Manchester was where American house music met British youth culture. London's warehouse scene gave birth to jungle and drum and bass. Both cities shaped the global spread of electronic music in the early 1990s.

Italy

Italy

An unlikely but essential node in the melodic techno story. Tale Of Us (Matteo Milleri & Carmine Stellato) brought a distinctly Italian sense of emotion and drama to techno's structure. Their Afterlife label has become one of the most influential in contemporary electronic music.

05 — Culture

More than music

The DJ as Author

Electronic music reframed what authorship means in music. A DJ does not compose, in the traditional sense. They select, sequence, and blend music made by others — but in doing so, they create something that did not exist before. The best DJs are not jukebox operators. They are editors of time, shapers of atmosphere, architects of collective experience.

The four-hour set at Berghain is not the same art form as the three-minute pop song. It requires different skills, different intentions, different listening. This is worth understanding before dismissing electronic music as "just pressing play."

The Floor as Common Ground

Electronic dance music has consistently been a space for people who did not fit elsewhere. Detroit techno emerged from Black communities ignored by mainstream America. Chicago house was built by Black and Latino gay men at a time when American society was actively hostile to their existence. Berlin's early techno scene absorbed people who had just watched the wall come down — a city of displacement and possibility.

This is not coincidence. The repetition, the darkness, the physical demands of extended dancing, the anonymity of clubs — these features create a particular kind of equality. On the floor, identity is temporarily suspended. That has always been the music's promise, and its politics.

Technology as Composition

In most musical traditions, the instrument is neutral — a guitar is a guitar before anyone plays it. In electronic music, the instrument is already half a composition. The specific character of a Roland TR-808 kick drum, the particular filter of a Moog synthesizer, the way an Ableton session behaves — these are not neutral choices. They shape what is possible to make.

This is why the history of electronic music is also a history of machines. And why, when a piece of equipment becomes obsolete, a sound often disappears with it — preserved only in records made while that machine was still in production.

The Long Night

Electronic music at its most serious is designed for duration. A Berghain set begins when most people are sleeping and ends when the light comes through windows that have been deliberately designed to prevent that from happening. The music works differently over four hours than it does over four minutes. It accumulates. It changes the body's relationship to time.

This is not something that translates easily to a playlist or a streaming service. It is an argument for being in a room, with other people, for a long time. That argument is worth taking seriously.
