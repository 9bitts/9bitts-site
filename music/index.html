01 — Origins

Where it began.

Electronic music is not a genre. It is a method — the use of electronic technology to generate, modify, or record sound. What began as avant-garde experimentation in European laboratories in the 1940s became, by the 1990s, the defining soundtrack of global youth culture.

The first machines were not built for clubs. They were built for laboratories. Composers like Pierre Schaeffer in Paris and Karlheinz Stockhausen in Cologne were interested in the philosophical problem of sound itself — what it meant to record reality and reassemble it. Musique concrète, the school Schaeffer founded in 1948, was the first systematic attempt to make music from recorded sound rather than performed sound. The tape recorder was the instrument.

For three decades the technology stayed expensive and institutional. Then it escaped. The synthesizer left the university, the drum machine left the studio, and a generation of young people — many of them poor, Black, queer, or otherwise pushed to the margins — discovered that these machines could build entire worlds. The history below is the story of that escape.

1948

Musique Concrète — Paris

Pierre Schaeffer experiments with recorded sounds at RTF studios. "Étude aux chemins de fer" — built from train sounds — becomes the first widely recognized piece of electronic music.

1957

The First Computer Music

At Bell Labs, Max Mathews writes MUSIC I, the first program to generate sound by computer. The idea that a machine could be told to make music — in code — is born here.

1964

The Moog Synthesizer

Robert Moog introduces voltage-controlled synthesis, making electronic sound production possible outside institutional studios. The synthesizer becomes an instrument a person can actually play.

1974

Kraftwerk — Autobahn

Four musicians from Düsseldorf release an album that sounds like nothing before it. Kraftwerk become the unlikely origin point of almost every genre in this guide — techno, house, hip-hop, synth-pop all trace back to them.

1977

Donna Summer — I Feel Love

Producer Giorgio Moroder builds an entire pop hit on a Moog sequencer. For the first time, a fully electronic track tops charts worldwide. The future of dance music is audible in a single record.

1980

Roland TR-808

A drum machine considered a commercial failure becomes the backbone of hip-hop, house, and techno. Its bass drum is still one of the most recognizable sounds in contemporary music.

1983

Chicago House

At The Warehouse and the Music Box, DJs Frankie Knuckles and Ron Hardy play a new kind of music assembled from disco, soul, and drum machines. The record stores label the bins "house." The name sticks.

1985

Detroit Techno

Juan Atkins, Derrick May, and Kevin Saunderson — the Belleville Three — fuse Kraftwerk's precision with funk's soul. They call it techno, after Alvin Toffler. A Black futurism for a collapsing industrial city.

1988

The Second Summer of Love

MDMA and Roland 303s transform British warehouse raves into a mass movement. Acid house explodes out of Chicago and into fields across England. A cultural rupture the establishment never saw coming.

1991

Berlin — Tresor Opens

In the vacuum left by the fallen Wall, Tresor opens in a former bank vault. Detroit and Berlin form an alliance across the Atlantic. The city begins its transformation into the global capital of techno.

1992

Jungle & the Hardcore Continuum

In London, breakbeats accelerate and bass deepens. Jungle — later drum and bass — emerges from rave's pirate-radio underground, the most rhythmically radical music Britain had produced.

2004

Berghain

A techno club opens in a former power plant in Berlin. Its uncompromising sound, marathon sets, and famously selective door make it the most mythologized nightclub on earth.

2010s

EDM & the American Explosion

A commercial, festival-scaled strain of dance music conquers the United States. Electronic music becomes, for the first time, the mainstream of global pop — for better and worse.

2010s–now

Melodic Techno & the Digital Era

Artists like Tale Of Us, Anyma, and Massano develop a more melodic, emotionally open strand of techno. Streaming and social media reshape how the music is discovered, sold, and experienced.

02 — Genres

The landscape of sound.

Electronic music is not one thing. It is a family of related approaches to rhythm, texture, and time — each with its own geography, its own tempo, its own emotional register.

Genres in electronic music are rarely clean categories. They blur, split, and recombine constantly. What follows is a map of the most significant territories — not a complete atlas, but enough to navigate by.

~120–130 BPM

House

Born in Chicago in the early 1980s from the ruins of disco. Four-on-the-floor kick, soulful vocals, chord stabs. The most democratic of electronic genres — built to make people move together.

Chicago, 1983 · Frankie Knuckles, Larry Heard
~130–150 BPM

Techno

Invented in Detroit as a sonic response to deindustrialization. Harder, more mechanical, more repetitive than house. Designed for endurance and transformation. The music of long nights.

Detroit, 1985 · Atkins, May, Saunderson
~124–128 BPM

Deep House

Slower, warmer, more introspective than regular house. Jazz chords, lush pads, understated rhythms. Music for the early morning or late night — the hours when the room empties but a few remain.

Chicago/New York, late 1980s · Larry Heard
~128–135 BPM

Melodic Techno

A recent evolution — techno's structure with deeper melodic and harmonic content. Emotional, often cinematic. The genre closest to the 9bitts aesthetic.

Italy/Germany, 2010s · Tale Of Us, Anyma
~125–132 BPM

Progressive House

Built around gradual evolution rather than sudden drops. Long builds, slow filters, a sense of perpetual motion. Shaped Sasha, Digweed, and later stadium-scale producers.

UK, early 1990s · Sasha, John Digweed
~120–125 BPM

Afro House

A rhythmically rich strain blending house with African percussion and melody. Now one of the fastest-growing global sounds, carried by labels like Keinemusik and artists like Black Coffee.

South Africa, 2010s · Black Coffee, &ME
~136–145 BPM

Trance

Hypnotic, layered synthesis with extended breakdowns designed to produce altered states. From German techno-trance of the 1990s to the modern psytrance scene — a vast, still-evolving territory.

Germany, early 1990s · Paul van Dyk, Sven Väth
~160–180 BPM

Drum & Bass

Breakbeats running at extreme speed over deep sub-bass. Born in London's rave underground. The genre that most directly confronted the raw physics of rhythm.

London, early 1990s · Goldie, LTJ Bukem
~118–125 BPM

Organic House

Downtempo, textural, built from acoustic and natural sounds woven into electronic frames. Music for sunrise sets and open landscapes rather than dark rooms.

2010s · Bedouin, Lee Burridge
~150–160 BPM

Hard Techno

Faster, heavier, more distorted. A return to techno's confrontational roots, dominating a new generation of European raves and festival floors.

Europe, revived 2020s · Sara Landry, I Hate Models
Variable

Ambient

Brian Eno codified it in 1978: music that could be ignored or actively listened to, working in both modes. The anti-genre — defined by absence as much as presence.

UK, 1978 · Brian Eno, Aphex Twin
~130–140 BPM

Minimal Techno

Stripped of everything non-essential. A single element, repeated with slight variation over long stretches. What remains when you remove the melody, the harmony, and most of the rhythm.

Berlin, late 1990s · Villalobos, Hawtin
03 — The Machines

The machines that made it.

Electronic music is inseparable from its instruments. Unlike acoustic music, where instruments predate the music by centuries, electronic music and its machines evolved together — each new device opening sonic possibilities that producers raced to explore.

What follows is not a list of gear. It is a list of objects that changed the sound of the world — often by accident, often after being declared failures by the companies that built them.

1964

Moog Synthesizer

Robert Moog's voltage-controlled synthesizer democratized electronic sound. Wendy Carlos's "Switched-On Bach" (1968) proved to a skeptical public that electronic instruments could make serious art. Everything after it carries Moog's DNA.

1980

Roland TR-808

A programmable drum machine considered a commercial failure — too artificial, too unlike real drums. Within a decade it became the foundation of hip-hop, house, and techno. Its kick is the most recognizable sound in modern music.

1981

Roland TB-303

Designed to simulate a bass guitar for solo practice. It failed at that. Chicago producers discovered that certain settings produced an alien, liquid, squelching sound unlike anything else. That sound became acid house.

1983

Yamaha DX7 & MIDI

The first affordable digital synth, and the arrival of MIDI — the protocol that let machines talk to each other. Suddenly a single musician could conduct an orchestra of devices. The modern studio is born.

1988

Akai MPC

A sampling drum machine and sequencer that became central to hip-hop and electronic production. The MPC's workflow — pads, sequences, samples — still shapes how producers think about rhythm today.

1994

Pioneer CDJ

The CD player that became the global club standard. CDJs redefined DJing — moving it from vinyl expertise toward real-time composition. The CDJ-3000 is the industry standard in serious clubs everywhere.

1997

Roland TB-303 reborn in software

As hardware aged, emulation began. The ReBirth software recreated the 303 and 808 on a home computer — the first sign that the bedroom would replace the studio. Production was about to become universal.

2001

Ableton Live

Software that changed the relationship between production and performance. Its session view made real-time arrangement possible. It became the tool of choice for a generation — including most artists shaping the current melodic techno scene.

When a machine becomes obsolete, a sound often disappears with it — preserved only in records made while it was still in production.

04 — Cities

Where the music lives.

Electronic music is not geography-neutral. Its key developments happened in specific places, at specific moments, under specific social conditions. Cities matter — not as scenery, but as cause.

Detroit's deindustrialization, Chicago's Black gay nightlife, Berlin's post-Wall vacancy — these were the soil in which the music grew. You cannot separate the sound from the streets that made it.

USA

Detroit

The birthplace of techno. The Belleville Three created the genre as a response to economic collapse — a vision of Black futurism that American capitalism had failed to deliver. Techno was, from the start, science fiction made audible.

USA

Chicago

Where house was born. The Warehouse, the Music Box, the Power Plant — Black and Latino gay clubs where DJs built a new form from disco's ruins. House was a survival culture before it was a global phenomenon.

Germany

Berlin

The fall of the Wall left a city full of empty space. Artists and DJs moved into abandoned buildings. Tresor, Berghain, Watergate became institutions. Berlin remains the global reference point for serious techno.

Spain

Ibiza

The Mediterranean island synonymous with summer electronic music. Amnesia, Pacha, DC-10 — clubs that shaped both the sound and the business of dance music globally. Contradictory, commercial, and still essential.

UK

Manchester & London

The Haçienda was where American house met British youth. London's warehouse scene birthed jungle and drum and bass. Together they drove the global spread of electronic music in the early 1990s.

Italy

Italy & Afterlife

An unlikely but essential node in the melodic techno story. Tale Of Us brought a distinctly Italian sense of emotion and drama to techno. Their Afterlife label became one of the most influential forces in contemporary electronic music.

05 — Culture

More than music.

To understand electronic music only as sound is to miss most of it. It is also a set of ideas about authorship, community, technology, and time — ideas worth taking seriously.

The DJ as author

Electronic music reframed what authorship means. A DJ does not compose in the traditional sense. They select, sequence, and blend music made by others — but in doing so, they create something that did not exist before. The best DJs are not jukebox operators. They are editors of time, shapers of atmosphere, architects of collective experience. The four-hour set is a different art form from the three-minute pop song. It requires different skills, different intentions, different listening.

The floor as common ground

Electronic dance music has consistently been a space for people who did not fit elsewhere. Detroit techno emerged from Black communities ignored by mainstream America. Chicago house was built by Black and Latino gay men at a time when society was actively hostile to their existence. Berlin's early scene absorbed people who had just watched a wall come down.

This is not coincidence. The repetition, the darkness, the physical demands of extended dancing, the anonymity of the club — these features create a particular kind of equality. On the floor, identity is temporarily suspended. That has always been the music's promise, and its politics.

Technology as composition

In most musical traditions the instrument is neutral — a guitar is a guitar before anyone plays it. In electronic music the instrument is already half a composition. The character of an 808 kick, the filter of a Moog, the way an Ableton session behaves — these are not neutral choices. They shape what is possible to make. This is why the history of electronic music is also a history of machines.

The long night

Electronic music at its most serious is designed for duration. A Berghain set begins when most people are sleeping and ends when light comes through windows deliberately designed to prevent it. The music works differently over four hours than over four minutes. It accumulates. It changes the body's relationship to time. This does not translate easily to a playlist or a streaming service. It is an argument for being in a room, with other people, for a long time — and that argument is worth taking seriously.

06 — Glossary

The vocabulary.

Every culture builds its own language. Here are the essential terms — enough to read a review, follow a conversation, or understand what a producer means when they talk about their work.

Four-on-the-floor
A steady kick drum on every beat of the bar. The rhythmic foundation of house and techno — the pulse that makes the music danceable.
BPM
Beats per minute — the tempo. House sits around 120–130, techno 130–150, drum & bass 160–180. BPM defines a genre as much as any instrument.
Drop
The moment of release after a build-up, when the full rhythm and bass return. In EDM it is the climax; in techno it is often deliberately understated or absent.
Build / Breakdown
The build raises tension by stripping and layering elements; the breakdown removes the drums entirely, leaving melody or atmosphere before the beat returns.
Beatmatching
Aligning the tempo and beats of two tracks so they play in sync. The fundamental DJ skill — done by ear before software could do it automatically.
Acid
The squelching, resonant sound of a Roland TB-303 with its filter modulated. It named an entire genre — acid house — and remains instantly recognizable.
Pad
A sustained, atmospheric synth sound that fills harmonic space. The lush background texture behind much of deep and melodic house.
Sample
A fragment of recorded sound reused in a new track. The building block of hip-hop and much electronic music — a vocal, a break, a single chord.
Sidechain
A production technique where the bass or pads "duck" in volume each time the kick hits, creating the pumping, breathing feel central to modern dance music.
Resident / B2B
A resident is a DJ tied to a particular club. B2B ("back to back") is two DJs sharing one set, trading records in turn.
