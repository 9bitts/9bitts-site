Nick Cave · A fé como escolha e nao como certeza

Faith as a Choice, Not a Certainty

Nick Cave doesn't believe in God the way some people believe in gravity. He chooses to. That distinction changes everything.

Nick Cave lost a son. Then another. And somewhere inside that, he kept writing songs that reach toward something sacred. Not because grief made God obvious. Because he decided to keep reaching anyway. That gap between loss and language, between silence and prayer, is where his particular kind of faith lives.

Most conversations about faith treat it as something you either have or you don't. A disposition, inherited or lost. But Cave has spoken and written about belief as something closer to an act of will. You choose to orient yourself toward meaning even when meaning doesn't arrive. That's not comfort. That's discipline.

There's something philosophically serious in that position. Kierkegaard called it a leap. William James called it a live hypothesis. But Cave doesn't dress it in academic language. He just keeps writing. The Red Hand Files, his open letter project, reads like a man thinking out loud about what it costs to believe when certainty is not available to you.

What this challenges is the modern assumption that doubt cancels faith. As if belief were only valid when bulletproof. But doubt and faith are not opposites. They share the same room. The person who has never doubted hasn't really chosen anything. They've just inherited a position and stayed inside it. Cave's faith is interesting precisely because it has been tested and he still shows up.

Maybe that's what faith actually is. Not the absence of doubt, but the decision to keep moving in a direction even when the ground doesn't confirm it. Certainty is passive. It just sits there. Choice is something you have to make again, every morning, against everything that tells you not to bother.
