Nick Cave · A fé como escolha e nao como certeza

Faith as a Decision, Not a Destination

Nick Cave doesn't believe because the evidence compels him. He believes because he keeps choosing to, again and again, in the dark.

Nick Cave has buried a son. Then another. And still he writes about God, not with the confidence of a man who has been answered, but with the persistence of one who keeps asking. That distinction matters more than most people want to admit. Faith, in his world, is not a reward for good reasoning. It is something closer to a daily act of will.

We tend to treat faith and certainty as the same thing, or at least as neighbors. But they may be opposites. Certainty closes. It settles the question and moves on. Faith, at its most honest, holds the question open and stays in the room with it. That requires something different from belief. It requires nerve.

Cave has spoken in interviews and in his Red Hand Files about doubt not as the enemy of faith but as part of its texture. He doesn't seem interested in resolving the tension. What he seems interested in is living inside it without collapsing. That is a harder practice than either pure belief or pure skepticism, and it is far less comfortable than either.

There is something useful in thinking of faith as a choice made without guarantees. It removes the passivity from the word. You are not waiting to be convinced. You are deciding, in the absence of proof, to orient yourself toward something rather than away from it. That orientation shapes what you notice, what you make, how you treat a Tuesday morning.

Cave keeps writing songs. Keeps answering letters from strangers. Keeps showing up to the grief and the making, without any promise that it adds up to something. Maybe that is the only definition of faith worth having: not the certainty that someone is listening, but the decision to speak anyway.
