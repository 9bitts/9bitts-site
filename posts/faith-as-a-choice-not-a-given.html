Nick Cave · A fé como escolha e nao como certeza

Faith as a Choice, Not a Given

Nick Cave doesn't believe because he has answers. He believes because he keeps choosing to, even when the ground gives way beneath him.

Nick Cave lost his son in 2015. He did not stop writing about God. That fact alone is worth sitting with, because most narratives about grief move toward either collapse or recovery. Cave moved toward something stranger: a faith that became more honest precisely because it no longer needed to protect itself from doubt.

There is a version of faith that functions as certainty dressed in religious clothing. It offers comfort by closing questions. Cave's relationship with belief seems to work the opposite way. In his letters and interviews, faith appears less like a settled position and more like a practice, something returned to daily, not because it resolves anything, but because the alternative feels like a kind of abandonment.

What makes this interesting philosophically is that it reframes faith as an act of will rather than an experience of conviction. You don't arrive at belief and stay there. You choose it again. That idea pulls belief out of the realm of the irrational and places it somewhere more uncomfortable: in the domain of decisions made without guarantees, like love, like making something, like showing up for another person when you have no proof it will matter.

Cave once described creativity itself as an act of faith, a reaching toward something that may or may not respond. That parallel is not decorative. If making art requires you to act as if meaning is possible before you have evidence that it is, then the artist and the believer are engaged in structurally similar work. Both are choosing, over and over, to treat the void as a conversation rather than a conclusion.

Maybe what Cave is pointing at is this: certainty doesn't require courage. Belief that survives proof isn't really belief, it's just knowledge with extra steps. But choosing faith in the presence of real doubt, after real loss, with full awareness that no answer is coming, that is something else entirely. It might be the most human thing a person can do.
